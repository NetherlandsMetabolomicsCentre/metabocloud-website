Metabocloud home

This is MetaboCloud a site that acts as an information medium on where to find metabolomics software and tools that were developed within the Netherlands Metabolomics Centre. Together the tools form a toolbox or data support platform for processing and storage of data from metabolomics studies.

The vision of the NMC is to make software and tools available for the international (metabolomics) community. 

The NMC toolbox contains two major components:

  1. a (metabolomics) study data capturing framework, and 
  2. data processing tools.

The first contains a repository of metabolomics data, facilitating storage of raw and processed (a.o.) metabolomics data and study information, which can be searched and queried.

The data processing tools cover most of the metabolomics data processing workflow, including data preprocessing, quality control, statistical analysis, metabolite identification and biological interpretation tools.

Data storage

Source: github.com/NetherlandsMetabolomicsCentre/GSCF

The general study capturing framework (GSCF) is an application that can store any biological study. It contains templates which makes it possible to personalize and / or standardize the data upload and storage.

In order to allow flexibility to capture all information you require within a study, and to make it possible to compare studies or study data, the system uses customizable templates and ontologies. It is especially designed to store complex study designs including cross-over designs and challenges.

In addition to the storage of study design data, data of so-called assays can be added, organized in assay modules.

One implementation of the GSCF provides access for guests, providing a way to explore the functionalities of the framework on a few public studies: studies.dbnp.org

The general study capturing framework facilitates sharing of data within a research group or consortium, as the study owner can decide who can view or access the data. In addition, the GSCF can stimulate collaborations by making study information publicly visible. New studies can be based on study data within the database, as standardized storage is stimulated by the system.

Processing software

As part of the Netherlands Metabolomics Centre a data processing infrastructure was developed, containing tools supporting the entire metabolomics workflow. Links to the tools that were developed in this context are gathered here, to provide one portal where these tools can be accessed. Most of the tools are also open source.

In the field of life sciences, LC-MS and GC-MS are among the most popular platforms to acquire data from human, microbial and plant tissues for food, nutrition and health applications. From these raw data, the metabolites can be derived that are most important and influential for a certain application. However, besides a proper data quality and analysis strategy, this also requires a preprocessing step that allows extracting information of pure components from the data.

mzExtract

The challenge with untargeted, global profiling metabolomics acquisition methods is the proper and automated extraction and integration of relevant metabolite features. mzExtract is a new algorithm that enables untargeted integration of samples that are measured with high resolution LC-MS. 

SOURCE: github.com/NetherlandsMetabolomicsCentre/mzExtract

mzQual

Hyphenated separation mass spectroscopy techniques are major platforms in metabolomics. A wide range of compounds can be measured (semi) quantitatively over a huge concentration range in a highly sensitive and selective manner. To obtain data of high quality (accurate, reproducible, high signal-to-noise), especially when a large number of samples have to be analysed, needs an elaborate quality control strategy.

The mzQual tool provides a fully automated control strategy, that can distinguish the various sources of variation. mzQual flags, and provides means for visual inspection of, suspicious samples or metabolites. Furthermore, the tool provides automated correction for different types of (unwanted) analytical variation, such as blank correction, internal standard correction and between- and within-batch correction. Finally, it provides figures of merit that quantify the original and final quality of the data.

SOURCE: github.com/NetherlandsMetabolomicsCentre/mzQual

Deconvolution is an approach to perform such tasks.

The starting point of this project is an existing tool for the deconvolution of GC-MS data (i.e. TNO-DECO, see Jellema et al, 2010. Chemometrics and intelligent laboratory systems 104: 132). Its unique building blocks are used to build a generic and automatic pre-processing tool for high resolution (HR) LC-MS and GC-MS data.

Simplivariate methods

Simplivariate methods can be used for an exploratory analysis of high-dimensional functional genomics data. It can be used in addition to Cluster Analysis and Principal Component Analysis. The advantage of simplivariate methods is their ability to generate simple and interpretable solutions. It is based on the observation that functional genomics data often contain both informative and non-informative variation, we propose a method that finds sets of variables containing informative variation. This informative variation is subsequently expressed in easily interpretable simplivariate components.

The informative variation is described by multiplicative models of the informative variables that can adequately represent the relations between functional genomics data.

The software and additional information can be downloaded from the website of the Biosystems Data Analysis group of the University of Amsterdam. Additionally, the Matlab® Genetic Algorithm and Direct search Toolboxes are required.

Identification

One major bottleneck in metabolomics is identification of all the metabolites observed in the biological samples measured. As a result the development of innovative identification methods is mandatory. Within the NMC multiple metabolite identification projects had been defined aimed at improving the current state of metabolite identification strategies. Main priorities of these research projects were the construction of MSn and NMR databases of reference metabolites, the development of new analytical methods (improved MSn acquisition, LC-MSn acquisition, automatic fractionation and enrichment of compounds present at low levels to obtain sufficient quantities for MSn and NMR measurements) and the development of innovative computational tools (MSn preprocessing and processing as well as NMR spectrum prediction, spectrum retrieval, visualization and NMR-based compound quantification tools). 
 
Important results were obtained with respect to databases: an advanced NMR/MS database has been constructed containing circa 6000 polyphenol/flavonoid compounds. In collaboration with the Humane Metabolome Database (David Wishart) a MSn spectral data base has been constructed consisting of circa 430 reference compounds. In addition, essential tools for MSn data preprocessing and processing have been created. Tool development has also been carried out for spectrum visualization of reference spectra present in the MSn and NMR databases as well as for structure annotation to unknown metabolites. One of the strategic projects in metabolite identification is computer-assisted identification by integration of a wide range of spectroscopic, physicochemical and biochemical data sources. Spectroscopic data are used for comprehensive structure generation followed by fast candidate rejection, aimed at leaving only a few candidate metabolites. 

MetiTree

URL: www.metitree.nl

Source: github.com/NetherlandsMetabolomicsCentre/metitree

MetiTree: a web application to organize and process high resolution multi-stage mass spectrometry metabolomics data.

Identification of metabolites using high resolution multistage mass spectrometry (MSn) data is a significant challenge demanding access to all sorts of computational infrastructures. MetiTree is a user-friendly, web application dedicated to organize, process, share, visualize, and compare MSn data. It integrates several features to export and visualize complex MSn data, facilitating the exploration and interpretation of metabolomics experiments. A dedicated spectral tree viewer allows the simultaneous presentation of three related types of MSn data, namely, the spectral data, the fragmentation tree, and the fragmentation reactions. MetiTree stores the data in an internal database to enable searching for similar fragmentation trees and matching against other MSn data. As such MetiTree contains much functionality that will make the difficult task of identifying unknown metabolites much easier.

 

REFERENCE:

MetiTree: a web application to organize and process high-resolution multi-stage mass spectrometry metabolomics data. Rojas-Chertó M., van Vliet M., Peironcely J.E., van Doorn R., Kooyman M., te Beek T., van Driel M.A., Hankemeier T., Reijmers T., Bioinformatics, Volume 28(20), (2012), pages 2707-2709.

MetIDB


URL: www.metidb.org

MetIDB is a reference database of experimental and predicted 1H NMR spectra of 6000 flavonoids. By incorporating the stereochemistry, intramolecular interactions, and solvent effects into the prediction model, chemical shifts and couplings were predicted with great accuracy. The reference database has a user-friendly web-based interface providing various interfaces to the data and data-mining possibilities. For each compound, additional information is available comprising compound annotation, a 1H NMR spectrum, 2D and 3D structure with correct stereochemistry, and monoisotopic mass as well as links to other web resources. Using this database, the process of flavonoid identification can then be significantly shortened by avoiding repetitive elucidation of already described compounds.

 

MetIDB: A Publicly Accessible Database of Predicted and Experimental 1H NMR Spectra of Flavonoids, Mihaleva VV, te Beek TAH, van Zimmeren F, Moco S, Laatikainen R, Niemitz M, Korhonen S-P, van Driel MA and J Vervoort, 85(18), 2013, 8700-8707


MEF

The Multistage Elemental Formula (MEF) tool allows assignment of correct elemental compositions to compounds, their fragment ions and neutral losses that originate from the molecular ion by using multistage mass spectrometry (MSn). MEF reduces the list of predicted elemental compositions for each ion by analyzing the elemental compositions of its parent (precursor ion) and descendants (fragments).

SAMSN

Multistage mass spectrometry (MSn) generating so called spectral trees is a powerful tool in the annotation and structural identification of metabolites and is increasingly used in the area of accurate mass LC/MS-based metabolomics to identify unknown compounds. As a consequence, there is a growing need for computational tools specifically designed for the processing and interpretation of MSn data.

The SAMSN (Structure Assignment MSn) tool is an extended version of the MEF tool. Besides processing of MSn spectral trees from fragmentation spectra into fragmentation trees (assignment of elemental compositions to all ions and neutral losses) it allows the extraction of MSn fragmentation tree fingerprints and the calculation of MSn fragmentation tree similarity values.  

 

SOURCE: github.com/NetherlandsMetabolomicsCentre/samsn

 

REFERENCE:

Metabolite identification using automated comparison of high-resolution multistage mass spectral trees. Rojas-Chertó M., Peironcely J.E., Kasper P.T., van der Hooft J.J.J., de Vos R.C.H., Vreeken R., Hankemeier T., Reijmers T., Analytical Chemistry, Volume 84(13), (2012), pages 5524-5534.

OMG

OMG (Open Molecule Generator) is the first open source structure generator which for a given elemental composition produces all non-isomorphic chemical structures that match that elemental composition. As additional input OMG can accept one or multiple (non-overlapping) prescribed substructures. As such OMG is especially of great importance for metabolomics, where identifying unknown metabolites is still a major bottleneck.  

SOURCE: sourceforge.net/projects/openmg/

REFERENCE:

OMG: Open Molecule Generator, Peironcely J.E., Rojas-Chertó M., Fichera D., Reijmers T., Coulier L., Faulon J.-L., Hankemeier T., Journal of Cheminformatics, Volume 4(September),  (2012), 21.

PMG

PMG (Parallel Molecule Generator) is a Java-based implementation of an open source multi-core automatic molecular structure generator, especially designed for metabolomics. It is an extended version of OMG, the earlier released structure generator. Due to improved speed it accommodates prescribed substructures and it removes unstable structures using a bad list of rings and substructures that are not found in human metabolites.

 

SOURCE: sourceforge.net/projects/pmg.

 

REFERENCE:

PMG: Multi-core metabolite identification., Jaghoori M.M., Jongmans S.T.Q., de Boer F., Peironcely J., Faulon J.-L., Reijmers T., Hankemeier T., Electronic Notes in Theoretical Computer Science, 299 (2013) 53-60.

Metabolite Likeness

In order to understand the chemical space spanned by endogenous metabolites in more detail human metabolites and non-metabolites were analyzed for chemical features that are characteristic for both classes of compounds. In addition it was checked which molecular descriptors and classifiers were capable of distinguishing metabolites from non-metabolites by assigning a 'metabolite-likeness' score.

SOURCE: sourceforge.net/projects/metabolikeness

REFERENCE:

Understanding and classifying metabolite space and metabolite-likeness., Peironcely J.E., Reijmers T., Coulier L., Bender A., Hankemeier T., PLoS ONE, Volume 6(12), (2011), e28966.

News02

What is Metabolomics

Much like genomics aims to unravel the structure of the genome, metabolomics focuses on understanding the many small molecule metabolites that result from a cell’s metabolic processes.

For research, the study of metabolomics provides the means to measure the effects of a variety of stimuli on individual cells, tissues, and bodily fluids.

By studying how their metabolic profiles change with the introduction of chemicals or the expression of known genes, for example, researchers can more effectively study the immediate impact of disease, nutrition, pharmaceutical treatment, and genetic modifications while using a systems biology approach.
